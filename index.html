Toast: Company & Industry Analysis

Peer-to-peer real-money gaming: market, competitors, regulation, and investment analysis. Toast (App Store listing Carnival) is a pre-seed startup that matches US players into live, casino-stakes blackjack against a real opponent, on the theory that facing another human keeps it a game of skill rather than gambling.

01 / Executive summary

Summary of findings

The load-bearing numbers, the case for and against, and the single unresolved question.

Toast is chasing a real-money market that is already large and growing fast: US iGaming (online casino) revenue reached $10.74B in 2025, up 27.6% year-over-year and the fastest-growing commercial-gaming segment in the country. That demand exists because only 8 states have legalized online casino gambling as of mid-2026, leaving an estimated ~300M people — about 88% of the US population — with no legal iCasino product to spend on, the structural gap Toast’s peer-to-peer model is built to reach. But the blackjack-realistic addressable footprint is closer to 32–37 states once operator-consensus exclusions, per-se card-game statutes, and hostile-doctrine states are subtracted — not the “40+” the pitch implies.

Toast is one of a small field, not the only entrant: the crowded “skill blackjack” apps are casual-stakes, asynchronous solitaire hybrids, VGW/Stake.us are house-banked, and a early crypto entrant, bjb.gg, is being built around exactly this live peer-to-peer real-money version. What is still uncontested is Toast’s specific combination: casino-level US-dollar stakes in an iOS consumer app. But the sector’s only public pure play, Skillz (rebranded Firy), trades down >99% from its 2021 peak at a distressed 0.68× EV/Sales, and each of the category’s three biggest players — Skillz, Papaya, AviaGames — has been hit by an integrity scandal, including Skillz’s $420M false-advertising verdict against Papaya.

Toast itself is barely funded, with a pre-seed round of just $1.6M, led by Lightspeed Venture Partners. Its founders are two former Mobile Premier League product leads.

The legal footing is thin: no US court has ever held blackjack skill-predominant, and California’s Penal Code §330 names “twenty-one” by statute as a banked game regardless of any skill analysis. Toast’s core mechanic — a paid offshore counterparty pool in India — has zero public corroboration and now sits on the wrong side of India’s 2025 Act, which criminalizes enabling online money-game financial transactions; an Apple Guideline-5.3.4 reinterpretation of “casino games” would be close to an extinction event for an iOS-only product, since Apple’s classification of skill-gaming apps as real-money gaming is a discretionary review call, not a bright line in the text.

Assessment: Toast is a bet that one specific combination — casino-stakes, real-time, peer-to-peer blackjack — unlocks a thinly populated position, one where the closest analogues are an emerging crypto entrant (bjb.gg) or casual-stakes async hybrids, and no one else yet pairs casino-level US-dollar stakes with an iOS consumer app. That bet holds only if three currently unproven things turn out true: that head-to-head blackjack survives state gambling doctrine, that the “another live human” disclosure never crosses the Papaya/AviaGames fault line, and that the paid-India counterparty model is both real and legally durable. The upside is that thinly populated position; the downside is that the category’s only public pure play is down more than 99% from its peak, and every one of the sector’s three biggest players has already hit an integrity scandal.

02 / The case for and against

The case for and against

A balanced read before the section-by-section detail: the strongest reasons to take Toast seriously, set against the strongest reasons for caution. This is a summary of the diligence, not investment advice.

+ Reasons to take it seriously

The bull case

  • A top-tier investor reportedly led the round. Lightspeed Venture Partners is named as lead on the pre-seed — a real signal that serious money did diligence. Caveat: the raise traces to a single company source (see the caution opposite).
  • Operators, not first-timers. Both founders are former Mobile Premier League product leads, and the CTO is a former Zupee engineer — direct real-money skill-gaming experience.
  • A real, shipping product. Live on iOS and Android, actively updated, with multiple games and a 4.59★ rating from roughly 951 iOS reviews. This is execution, not a pitch deck.
  • A large, genuinely underserved market. US iGaming revenue reached $10.74B in 2025, the fastest-growing commercial-gaming segment, yet about ~300M people — ~88% of the US — have no legal online-casino option.
  • A B2B path that spreads the risk. Toast licenses its game stack and player network to operators (Sparket, GamerSaloon), not only a consumer app — a route that can scale away from pure consumer regulatory exposure.
  • A thin, early niche. Casino-stakes, real-time, peer-to-peer card play delivered as a consumer app is a sparsely populated position today.

− Reasons for caution

The bear case

  • A user-facing metric is confirmed fabricated. The in-app referral “live earnings” feed is a canned loop — the same names and amounts repeat, and earnings do not scale with referral count. A willingness to deceive users is a governance signal, not just a bug.
  • The player counts are almost certainly inflated. About 9,000 concurrent “active players” against ~10,000 Android installs and ~951 iOS ratings is not credible.
  • The whole legal theory rests on one unresolved question. “Skill, not gambling” holds only if opponents are real humans; whether real-money matches use bots is unresolved, and settleable only by an on-device test. If it is bots, the theory weakens toward the AviaGames “acted as the house” finding.
  • Untested legal theory, heavy regulatory exposure. No US court has held blackjack skill-predominant; California’s Penal Code §330 names “twenty-one” by statute; Apple classifies the app “Gambling”; and India’s 2025 Act criminalizes enabling money-game transactions.
  • Claims are uncorroborated and inconsistent. The $1.6M raise is single-origin (Tracxn lists the company “Unfunded”), and the pitch cites “$500M ARR” against “$72M” on the company’s own site.
  • The comparables are cautionary. The category’s only public pure-play, Skillz, is >99% off its peak, and each of the sector’s biggest players has hit an integrity scandal — including Skillz’s $420M verdict against Papaya.

The honest balance. Two issues are dealbreaker-class: a demonstrated willingness to fabricate a user-facing metric (the referral feed, confirmed) and the unresolved bot question the legal theory depends on. Several scarier-sounding leads did not hold up on inspection — the US-plus-India structure is a standard “Delaware flip,” the “Math.random opponent” belongs to practice mode rather than the cash match, and a large paid human pool is economically impossible on a $1.6M raise. 03 / Mechanics

How the product works

A US player taps Play. In well under a second, Toast's own "matching engine" — the founder's term for it, relayed on the diligence — seats them against a live opponent at a shared dealer's table. What the company account describes, and what the company itself has confirmed, are two different things: step through the five states below, then see the company section for exactly where that line falls.

Step 1 of 5

1. The US player taps Play

A US player opens Carnival, Toast's real-money app — live on the App Store since July 2025 — and taps Play on a blackjack table.

2. The matching engine pairs an offshore counterpart

Per the founder's own description on the diligence, Toast's matching engine instantly pairs the US player with a member of a paid offshore player pool in India — invisible to the user, who just knows it's another live human.

3. Both play the same dealt hand

The two players are dealt the identical hand by the identical dealer at the same table — technically playing each other, not the dealer, which is exactly the mechanic the next step's legal argument rests on.

4. Legal framing: player vs. player = game of skill

Because each player is nominally playing the other, not "the house," the activity is classed a game of skill rather than casino gambling in jurisdictions that recognize the distinction — the load-bearing legal argument behind the whole model. See the seam it operates in for where this holds and where it doesn't.

5. The economics: who pays whom

In the described model, the US player might wager $10 on the hand while the matched Indian player is staking $0.10 — India pay reportedly around $1/hour. Toast takes a rake from the pot; the US player, not the India-based counterpart, is the one being monetized.

What this depicts: the matching mechanic exactly as described on the intro call and relayed from the founder — not an independently confirmed product flow. The paid-offshore-player mechanics and the stake figures have no public corroboration found despite a real search; see the company section below, specifically "What we could not verify," for the full verification status of every claim in this diagram.

05 / Loophole

Regulatory and legal analysis

Toast’s legality is not one question but fifty-one. Only eight jurisdictions have legal regulated iCasino — a market worth $10.73B in 2025 across the seven that were live — and the pitch reads the rest as open field. The verified map below says the honest blackjack-addressable count is nearer 32–37 states, and the industry’s own Terms & Conditions mark exactly where the seam is thinnest.

The three legal tests

Whether an entry-fee, cash-prize game is “gambling” is decided state by state, under one of three tests. Which test a state uses matters more to Toast than anything in its pitch deck.

Test 1 · majority rule

Predominance

Is the outcome driven more by skill than by chance? The majority rule — more than 30 states, including CA, GA, IL, MI, NC, OH and PA — and the test the entire skill-gaming industry is built on. New Jersey applies it as the “predominant purpose” test (O’Brien v. Scott). Toast’s P2P design is an argument only here.

Test 2 · stricter

Material element

Even a mostly-skill game is gambling if chance still matters. New York’s Penal Law §225.00 catches any game whose outcome “depends in a material degree upon an element of chance” — skill can dominate and the game still loses. Applied by Missouri and New York; Skillz’s own legal explainer counts 8 states. A card draw is very hard to argue below “material.”

Test 3 · strictest

Any chance

A modicum of chance makes the game illegal. The strictest reading, applied by Arizona, Arkansas, Iowa, Louisiana and Tennessee — Arizona’s statute sweeps in contests “of chance or skill” alike. No matching engine or P2P structure changes what a card flip is.

Outliers ignore skill entirely. South Carolina, Maryland and Michigan ask only whether a wager was placed; Washington makes internet gambling a class C felony; Arkansas criminalizes betting on “any game of hazard or skill”; Florida prohibits wagering on contests of skill; Montana bans all commercial online gambling regardless of skill; Hawaii reaches whatever stirs the “gambling instinct”. There is no federal safe harbor either: UIGEA defines a bet as staking value on “a game subject to chance” and makes internet gambling unlawful wherever state law makes the bet unlawful — federal exposure piggybacks on whichever state Toast gets wrong. And the honesty caveat from a firm in this business: “ask 3 lawyers for such a list and you may get 3 different lists”.

The lead product, specifically

Why blackjack is a distinct legal risk

The research found no U.S. authority holding blackjack skill-predominant, in any format — gaming-law commentary instead uses it as the textbook game that fails the any-chance test: chance card flips, skill hit-or-stand decisions. Three statutes single cards out by name or per se: California Penal Code §330 criminalizes “twenty-one” among banking and percentage card games; Indiana deems a card game, or an electronic version of one, “a game of chance” that “may not be considered a bona fide contest of skill”; Maine counts as chance whatever skill “can not be eliminated” — and no skill eliminates a card draw. South Carolina criminalizes betting at “any game with cards or dice”.

Enforcement has already reached a blackjack app: the Michigan Gaming Control Board’s October 2024 cease-and-desist named Papaya’s 21 Cash — blackjack — as illegal unlicensed internet gaming. The industry prices the card line in its own Terms: Skillz and AviaGames independently bar card games in the identical two states (ME and IN for Skillz; IN and ME for Avia), and Triumph bars cards in six states. Toast’s answer is design: 21 Hustle is marketed as head-to-head blackjack with “no dealer or RNG edge” — a genuine predominance argument, and an untested one, aimed at the one large state that names the game in its penal code.

Where operators exclude cash play, in their own terms

A state exclusion list in an operator’s own Terms is priced-in legal judgment — the industry’s revealed preference. Five operators publish lists. Skillz markets real-prize play in only 40 US states by its own count; Toast’s consumer Carnival Terms concede five states (AZ, SC, MI, IA, LA) while its B2B platform ToS publishes no state list at all and its App Store listing is silent on states. Louisiana — the only state all five exclude — backs that consensus with a dedicated “gambling by computer” felony for operators.

Five ways a prosecutor could attack the model

Tier D · analysis Summary of public sources for internal research; not legal advice.

The diligence framing asks exactly this: “if I was a prosecutor…” Each argument below is grounded in a cited precedent; each is paired with the strongest structural defense.

  1. Economic substance: the player is really playing the house

    Because Toast recruits, pays and controls the offshore counterparty, the US player is arguably facing house-supplied liquidity — player-vs-house gambling dressed as P2P. AviaGames shows fact-finders will look through the “human-vs-human” label: it matched players against company-controlled bots designed to beat them, and drew a federal grand jury probe.

    Strongest defense — AviaGames’ trigger was non-human bots plus affirmative denials; Toast’s opponents are real humans exercising real skill. Disclose that matching may be offshore human labor and the case’s actual trigger — misrepresentation — is absent.

  2. “A scheme intended to evade the law”

    The NY AG’s DFS frame fits verbatim: DraftKings and FanDuel were called “the leaders of a massive, multi-billion-dollar scheme intended to evade the law” in the 2015 cease-and-desist letters. An offshore counterparty recruited solely to manufacture a second “player” reads as intent evidence.

    Strongest defense — structuring around legal lines is what lawful tax and corporate planning is; design-for-compliance is not fraud. And the DFS story ended with New York legalizing DFS as a game of skill nine months later — the letter opened a negotiation, not a conviction.

  3. Illegal gambling in substance, whatever the skill label

    UIGEA did not exclude games of skill at the payments layer, and Black Friday shows the endgame: the Scheinberg indictment stacked UIGEA violations with bank fraud and money laundering and sought roughly $3B. In the ~40 states without legal iCasino, cash blackjack simply looks like unlawful gambling under state law.

    Strongest defense — the state test governs, and under the majority predominance rule head-to-head blackjack between two humans — where the better decision-maker wins over time — has a genuine claim that no house-banked game is being offered at all.

  4. Consumer deception about the counterparty

    Papaya told users over 200 times that it did not use bots while a court later found bot use “undisputed”; the price was a $420M false-advertising verdict plus a $15M consumer settlement. Presenting a salaried, sub-dollar-an-hour worker as an ordinary “live human” opponent — without disclosing the house pays them — invites the same material-omission theory.

    Strongest defense — Papaya’s liability flowed from affirmative lies about hidden bots. If Toast never lies and never fields a bot, “another live human” is true; the fix is disclosure and UX discipline, not a different model.

  5. AML and money transmission

    Toast moves US player money in and India payout money out. A business transferring funds is an MSB “regardless of the amount” with mandatory AML programs, and practitioner guidance puts fund-handling gaming platforms squarely in AML scope. India now separately prohibits enabling online-money-game financial transactions. Black Friday is the template for charging the flows, not the game.

    Strongest defense — these are compliance obligations, not inherent illegality: register, build real KYC/AML, use licensed payment partners, and pay counterparties as payroll/contractors rather than gaming payouts. The risk is execution and cost. It is also the one argument with no structural fix — and the one a federal prosecutor is best resourced to pursue.

The through-line: arguments 1–4 share a single structural defense — never misrepresent the counterparty, and never let the counterparty become a bot or a pure house cutout. Argument 5 is a build-and-spend problem that exists regardless.

Regulatory timeline

Workaround gaming models get a gray-zone grace period, then one of three endings: legalization (the DFS playbook), enforcement (online poker’s Black Friday, the sweepstakes wave, India), or reclassification under a friendlier regulator (Kalshi’s CFTC-preemption win — though district courts remain split). The sweepstakes path is the live warning: five states banned the model in 2025 and California’s AB 831 extends liability to suppliers and platform providers — a structure that would reach a matching-and-liquidity provider like Toast, not just a consumer brand.

India: the labor pool’s legal exposure Tier D · analysis

Toast’s counterparty supply sits on collapsing ground. India first flattened its own skill/chance line with a 28% GST on the full face value of real-money gaming deposits (2023), then erased it: the Promotion and Regulation of Online Gaming Act, 2025 prohibits “online money games” and treats established games of skill as prohibited, with penalties up to 3 years’ imprisonment and a ₹10M fine for offering one, plus an advertising ban and a bar on any person enabling such games or their financial transactions.

Do Toast’s paid players fall inside the ban? On the most Toast-favorable reading, a wage to play is labor, not staking one’s own money — so the individual worker is arguably not a “player” of an “online money game.” But the platform side is exposed as an enabler of game-linked financial flows, the skill safe harbor is gone, and cross-border pay raises FEMA and labor-classification questions of its own. Net: a deteriorating counterparty source, with constitutional challenges pending before India’s Supreme Court.

08 / Cautionary tales

Precedents and cautionary cases

The category’s only public pure-play is down more than 99% from its peak, and its three biggest names have each been hit by an integrity scandal: a finding that the game was not what players were told. That is the exact seam Toast’s “another live human” model runs along.

The pattern, in two verdicts

Two precedents on the same question: was the opponent real?

Neither company was killed by the market. Each was found to have deceived players about who — or what — they were playing against. That is the integrity question, and it is the one Toast’s paid-counterparty model sits directly on top of.

AviaGames · Pocket7Games

AviaGames: from civil verdict to criminal probe

$42.9M willful-infringement jury verdict (Feb 2024)
$80M total settlement ($50M upfront + royalties)
Grand jury active DOJ criminal probe into bot use

A California federal jury found Pocket7Games willfully infringed Skillz patents, with trial evidence of internal communications about matching real players against bots. The parties then settled for around $80M, but the more serious exposure is criminal: Skillz disclosed a DOJ criminal grand jury subpoena examining whether bot-matching constitutes fraud or illegal gambling. This is the prosecutor hypothetical, already realized against a directly analogous operator.

Papaya Gaming

Papaya: a $420M Lanham Act verdict

$420M false-advertising verdict (Apr 23, 2026)
$15M consumer class-action settlement (Oct 2025)

A Southern District of New York jury awarded Skillz $420 million — reportedly the largest Lanham Act false-advertising award in US history — after finding Papaya misled users into believing they competed against other humans. Separately, Papaya took a $15M consumer class-action settlement over the same undisclosed-bots allegations, and Michigan regulators had already issued a cease-and-desist declaring its products illegal gambling under state law. Same defect as AviaGames: the honesty about the opponent was the problem.

Read together, the sector’s failures were not market failures — they were integrity failures: findings that players were deceived about who they were playing against. That is the exact surface Toast’s model touches — a paid, company-controlled offshore counterparty pool sits precisely where “another live human” either holds as a defense or collapses into the same deception the courts have already punished.

10 / Economics

Unit economics

Toast's revenue is a rake on a peer-to-peer pot, not a house edge. It is the same mechanic as poker or daily fantasy. Four questions decide whether that math compounds: how the rake actually works; what the category's only public pure-play looks like across a full decade; whether casino-style play really lifts lifetime value the way the pitch assumes; and whether two private app-store gatekeepers can end the business with a policy interpretation, no court required.

How the rake works

Skillz's S-1 (the only skill-gaming filing that defines the mechanic in writing) describes Take Rate as the share of entry fees the platform keeps after paying out the winner: structurally a rake on a shared prize pool, not a fixed statistical edge against the player. Toast's blackjack product is built on the same entry-fee mechanic. Skillz's own revenue-over-GMV math implies a take rate that rose from 14.5% (2020) to 19.3% (2025), above DFS's typical 10–15% and well above online poker's 2–10% of the pot, consistent with a platform that also has to fund its own acquisition spend out of that same rake.

The LTV argument: does casino-style play lift lifetime value

The underwriting bet is that skill-gaming's historically low LTV reflects a latency problem rather than a failure of casino-style games to monetize better. Once play is fast and casino-shaped, LTV should look more like a casino or poker player than a casual-game player. The public evidence is genuinely mixed, so we present it as a ledger rather than a verdict.

Against the thesis

  • Skillz's own paying user base fell ~77% from its 2021 peak (510K) to 2024 (118K), while ARPPU stayed flat in a narrow $59–70 band the whole time. If solving latency alone were enough to unlock casino-style LTV, Skillz (which explicitly chased faster, more skill-intensive titles as it scaled) should show rising ARPPU. It does not.
  • No Skillz cohort-retention curve, LTV:CAC ratio, or payback-period figure was found disclosed anywhere in the public record. This is a genuine evidence gap: the best available public data point is the aggregate PMAU/ARPPU history above, not a cohort-level number.

For the thesis

  • DraftKings' licensed iGaming generates roughly 2.3× the revenue per population-point of its own Sportsbook, direct evidence that casino-style play monetizes a reached population at a multiple of the adjacent, proven sports-betting market.
  • A widely-cited comparison found an 8×–87× monetization gap between real-money poker ARPU and casual free-to-play games; mobile user-acquisition markets separately price casino-genre app installs at roughly 15× the CPI of casual apps, an indirect market-pricing signal that buyers underwrite casino-app users as far higher-LTV.

Net assessment: the thesis has real market-pricing support, but the one company that actually tried to fix the latency problem at scale (Skillz) did not show rising per-user monetization as a result. This argues that Toast's dealer-vs-player mechanic would need to be doing real incremental work, not just "a faster Skillz," for the bull case to hold.

Platform-gatekeeper risk

Two platforms can delist, with no appeal

Both Apple and Google draw a discretionary line between "real money gambling" (heavily gated) and other real-money contest formats. Whether a skill-classified, dealer-vs-player blackjack app reads as a "casino game" requiring a gambling license, or as an exempt "skill contest," is a review-team judgment call, not a bright line in the guideline text. Carnival is iOS-only with no web-distribution fallback comparable to Skillz's website-plus-app presence, so a delisting is close to an extinction event, not a growth setback.

"5.3.4 Apps that offer real money gaming (e.g. sports betting, poker, casino games, horse racing) or lotteries must have necessary licensing and permissions in the locations where the app is used, must be geo-restricted to those locations, and must be free on the App Store." Apple App Review Guidelines §5.3.4
"Apps that have navigational elements or features … that provide a 'call to action' to wager, stake, or participate in real-money games, contests or tournaments using real money … in a tournament for a chance to win a cash prize" are treated as policy violations outside Google's five licensed real-money categories. Google Play Real-Money Gambling, Games, and Contests policy

The word "casino games" is explicitly listed as an example requiring a gambling license under Apple's 5.3.4; a skill-classified blackjack app sits right on that boundary. Apple takes no cut of Carnival's cash deposits (no IAP on cash deposits), which is a margin advantage today but also exactly why delisting (not fee extraction) is the lever Apple actually holds. This is the platform-policy mirror of the state-gambling-doctrine risk elsewhere in this brief: Toast faces the same binary, discretionary risk from two private companies that it faces from state prosecutors, with even less procedural recourse.

11 / Thesis

Investment conclusion

Three cases, one honest number range, and the 26 questions we'd put to the founders before wiring anything. The bull case tops out at a defensible ~$0.6B derived, not the DraftKings-parity ~$0.9B stretch some pitches imply. The bear case is not a haircut to the base case; it is a step-change toward impairment if any one of four precedented kill-shots lands.

Thesis summary

Toast is a bet that one specific combination (casino table-game formats, at casino stakes, matched in real time, wrapped in the peer-to-peer "game-of-skill" legal theory) unlocks the ~32–37 states that plausibly have no legal iCasino product for cash blackjack specifically, out of the ~42–43 states (~300M people) with no iCasino product at all. No other researched competitor occupies that casino-stakes × real-time quadrant (market map): Skillz/Triumph are real-time but casual-stakes, PrizePicks is P2P but async, VGW/Stake.us are real-time casino but house-banked. The founders are ex-Mobile Premier League product leads (MPL background) with a $1.6M Lightspeed pre-seed (Lightspeed portfolio page), and per-hand economics show the offshore-India labor line is a rounding error, roughly 0.3–1.7% of gross rake and robust to a 10× cost shock (per-hand model), so the real asset is instant liquidity, not labor arbitrage. The thesis holds only if three things that are currently unproven are true: that head-to-head blackjack survives state gambling doctrine (no US court has ever held blackjack skill-predominant, and California names "twenty-one" by statute); that the disclosure around the paid opponent never crosses the line that cost Papaya a $420M verdict and put AviaGames in front of a federal grand jury; and that the paid-India counterparty model (which has zero public corroboration anywhere) is both real and legally durable even as India's 2025 Act criminalizes enabling money-game transactions. The upside is a genuinely empty quadrant; the downside is that the closest public pure-play, Skillz, is down >99% from its peak and every one of the sector's three biggest players hit an integrity scandal.

How the model is built

Each case extends the three-case framing above into a Year 1–5 build. ARPPU is a monthly figure on the Skillz basis, so annual net revenue = paying users (K) × ARPPU × 12. GMV = net revenue ÷ take rate. EBITDA-ish = net revenue × (1 − S&M% − 40%), where the 40% is a single simplifying charge for all other opex (payments, platform fees, G&A, tech); the India counterparty labour line is immaterial to unit economics (per-hand model).

Ramp curves (state rollout, user growth) are the scenario assumption. The bull compounds into the casino × real-time quadrant with ARPPU breaking above the band, echoing DraftKings iGaming's 2.3× revenue density over sportsbook; the base is a niche operator with ARPPU stuck in the 14.5–19.3%-rake, $59–70-ARPPU Skillz reality; the bear takes a Year-3 enforcement, platform or India shock. Additional considerations

  • India's own ban reaches the labor pool. The counterparty supply is not a stable input: India's 2025 Act erased the skill/chance distinction and criminalizes enabling online money games or their financial transactions (up to 3 yrs / ₹10M), which can reach a platform routing US-linked money to Indian players even if the individual workers are treated as paid labor, not wagerers.
  • Card-network rules quietly favor the model. Skill-cash apps process under MCC 5816 ("games of skill"), VIRP Tier 2, materially less restrictive than gambling's MCC 7995. And Apple takes no IAP cut on cash deposits, a margin advantage, but also exactly why delisting, not fee extraction, is the lever Apple holds.
  • Operator T&C exclusions are de-facto law. What five operators geo-block in their own terms is the industry's priced-in legal judgment. Carnival's list is short (AZ, SC, MI, IA, LA) and under-excludes vs. peer consensus: it serves AR, TN, CT, DE, SD. The diligence question is no longer "is there a list" but "why is Toast's priced-in risk appetite larger than every peer except Papaya, the one that got the C&D?"
  • The founder's own "$6B market" number doesn't match any construction we can reproduce. Toast's co-founder publicly pegs US skill-based real-money gaming at "$6 billion … with legal clarity in over 40 states." That figure carries no stated methodology and sits ~3× below the AGA-measured $18.6B illegal-iGaming SAM yet ~15× above the top of the comp-anchored $50–400M SOM; and "legal clarity in over 40 states" overstates the 32–37-state blackjack-realistic range. Ask the founder to source both halves of the quote.
  • Comp-basis note (open contradiction, handled). Skillz's 2021 peak is stated two ways on two different bases, and both are correct: ~$16B at the February 2021 intraday/early-year peak ($874.40 split-adjusted ATH) vs. ~$3.04B at the December 31, 2021 year-end close.   • Exit path is thin. No DraftKings/FanDuel-direct acquisition of a pure P2P skill-gaming company exists; the only precedents are a PE roll-up (Game Taco/WorldWinner) and Skillz's own internal pivot away from the category (into ad-tech), a category whose leader diversified out rather than winning it.
  • Name-collision is a live operational risk. A financial-news outlet already misattributed Toast Studios' Sparket deal to ticker TOST (restaurant POS), a real, published disambiguation risk.
